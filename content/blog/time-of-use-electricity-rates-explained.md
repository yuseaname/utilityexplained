<h2 id="what-are-time-of-use-electricity-rates">What Are Time of Use Electricity Rates?</h2>
Time of Use (TOU) electricity rates explained simply: they charge you different prices for electricity depending on <em>when</em> you use it. Instead of one flat rate per kilowatt-hour (kWh), your utility splits the day into time periods — each with its own price.
          This system reflects the real cost of generating and delivering electricity. Power plants (especially natural gas “peaker” plants) that run only during high-demand hours are expensive to operate. Utilities pass some of that cost to you through higher prices during those times.
          Most residential TOU plans include three periods:
          <ul>
          <li><strong>Peak hours:</strong> Highest demand and highest price (e.g., 2–7 pm on weekdays)</li>
          <li><strong>Off-peak hours:</strong> Lowest demand and lowest price (e.g., 9 pm–9 am daily, plus weekends)</li>
          <li><strong>Partial-peak (or mid-peak) hours:</strong> Intermediate cost (e.g., 12–2 pm and 7–9 pm on weekdays)</li>
          </ul>
          For example, in California under PG&E’s TOU-E plan in 2026, rates might be:
          <ul>
          <li>Peak: 42.3¢/kWh</li>
          <li>Partial-peak: 28.5¢/kWh</li>
          <li>Off-peak: 14.6¢/kWh</li>
          </ul>
          That’s a 280% difference between cheapest and most expensive times. The exact numbers vary by utility, region, and year — but the structure is similar nationwide.
          TOU plans are becoming more common as utilities install smart meters and shift demand management responsibilities to customers. In 2026, over 35% of U.S. residential customers will be offered a TOU option — up from 18% in 2020 (U.S. EIA data).

<h2 id="how-tou-rates-work-in-practice">How TOU Rates Work in Practice</h2>
A typical weekday under a TOU plan might look like this:
          - <strong>Off-peak:</strong> 9:00 pm – 9:00 am (12 hours)
          - <strong>Partial-peak:</strong> 12:00 pm – 2:00 pm and 7:00 pm – 9:00 pm (5 hours)
          - <strong>Peak:</strong> 2:00 pm – 7:00 pm (5 hours)
          Weekends and holidays are usually all off-peak.
          Your smart meter tracks usage by the hour and applies the correct rate. You’ll see breakdowns on your monthly bill — often in a table or chart. Some utilities even offer mobile apps that show real-time rate periods and projected costs.
          Let’s say you ran your dishwasher at 6:00 pm (peak). If it uses 1.2 kWh, and peak rates are 40¢/kWh, that cycle costs 48¢. The same dishwasher run at 10:00 pm (off-peak) at 15¢/kWh would cost only 18¢ — a 62% savings on that load.
          That’s the core idea: <strong>shifting usage</strong> to lower-cost hours reduces your bill — if you’re willing to adjust habits or use smart devices.

<h2 id="who-benefits-most-from-tou-plans">Who Benefits Most From TOU Plans?</h2>
Not everyone saves money on TOU plans — but certain households see real benefits.
          <strong>Best candidates include:</strong>
          <ul>
          <li>Homeowners who work outside the home during peak hours (2–7 pm) and return after 7 pm</li>
          <li>People who run major appliances (dishwasher, washing machine, dryer) after 7 pm or before 2 pm</li>
          <li>Electrical vehicle (EV) owners who charge overnight (off-peak) instead of mid-afternoon</li>
          <li>Households with solar panels — TOU can maximize self-consumption of solar energy during midday partial-peak hours</li>
          </ul>
          In contrast, TOU may <strong>cost more</strong for:
          <ul>
          <li>Retirees or remote workers who run appliances during midday or early evening</li>
          <li>Families with young children who use high-wattage devices (e.g., oven, HVAC) during peak hours</li>
          <li>Households with high baseline electricity use unrelated to scheduling</li>
          </ul>
          A 2025 study by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau found that 61% of households switching to TOU saw lower bills, but only if they actively adjusted usage. The average savings: $137/year. The top 25% of savers cut bills by over 22%.
          <h3>The Role of Smart Thermostats and Timers</h3>
          Smart home tech makes TOU planning effortless.
          - A smart thermostat can pre-cool your home before peak hours, then raise the temperature slightly during 2–7 pm — using stored “coolth” instead of running the AC during expensive periods.
          - Water heaters with timers can run just before 2 pm or after 9 pm, not during peak.
          - EV chargers with smart scheduling can start at 10 pm, avoiding even partial-peak.
          These tools don’t require lifestyle changes — just initial setup. And many utilities offer rebates for enrolling in demand-response programs (e.g., PG&E’s SmartAC program saves customers $75/year on average).

<h2 id="how-tou-rates-save-money-a-real-world-ex">How TOU Rates Save Money: A Real-World Example</h2>
Let’s walk through a real household’s before-and-after comparison.
          <strong>Baseline (Standard Flat Rate Plan):</strong>
          - 900 kWh/month used
          - Rate: 22.5¢/kWh
          - Monthly bill: $202.50
          <strong>Same household on TOU plan (2026 PG&E-style rates):</strong>
          - 300 kWh during off-peak (15.2¢/kWh)
          - 250 kWh during partial-peak (27.8¢/kWh)
          - 350 kWh during peak (39.6¢/kWh)
          - Total: (300 × 0.152) + (250 × 0.278) + (350 × 0.396) = $227.90
          Wait — that’s <em>higher</em>. But what if they shift just 100 kWh from peak to off-peak?
          - Off-peak: 400 kWh
          - Partial-peak: 250 kWh
          - Peak: 250 kWh
          - Total: (400 × 0.152) + (250 × 0.278) + (250 × 0.396) = $202.60
          Still only break-even. Now shift 200 kWh:
          - Off-peak: 500 kWh
          - Partial-peak: 250 kWh
          - Peak: 150 kWh
          - Total: (500 × 0.152) + (250 × 0.278) + (150 × 0.396) = $177.90
          → **$24.60/month saved ($295/year)**
          The key insight: <strong>shifting 10–15% of peak usage to off-peak can generate savings in most cases.</strong> That’s often achievable with small adjustments — like running the dishwasher after 9 pm instead of at 6 pm.

<h2 id="common-mistakes-to-avoid-with-tou-rates">Common Mistakes to Avoid With TOU Rates</h2>
Even well-intentioned customers sometimes make choices that increase their bills on TOU plans.
          <strong>Mistake 1: Assuming “all evening is off-peak”</strong>
          Many people think 6–9 pm is cheap — but it’s often <strong>partial-peak</strong> (or peak in some plans). Always verify your utility’s exact hours.
          <strong>Mistake 2: Running multiple high-wattage appliances simultaneously during peak</strong>
          A 4,500-watt water heater + 1,800-watt oven + 1,500-watt AC = 7,800W. At 40¢/kWh, that’s $3.12 <em>per hour</em>.
          <strong>Mistake 3: Ignoring seasonal shifts</strong>
          In summer, peak may start earlier (1 pm instead of 2 pm) and last longer (until 8 pm). Your winter pattern may not apply.
          <strong>Mistake 4: Not reviewing bills monthly</strong>
          Smart meters let you track usage by hour. Use that data to adjust — e.g., if your peak usage is >35% of total, you’re likely overspending.
          <h3>Pro Tips for TOU Success</h3>
          <ul>
          <li><strong>Set timers for major appliances:</strong> Dishwashers, washing machines, pool pumps.</li>
          <li><strong>Pre-cool or pre-heat:</strong> Lower AC temperature before 2 pm so the system runs less during peak.</li>
          <li><strong>Charge EVs overnight:</strong> Most EVs charge 3–4× faster (and cheaper) off-peak.</li>
          <li><strong>Batch cooking:</strong> Roast a chicken and use the oven’s residual heat to warm food — no extra power needed.</li>
          </ul>

<h3>1. Are TOU rates always cheaper than flat rates?</h3>
          No — not automatically. TOU is a <em>structural</em> change, not a discount. Savings depend entirely on <em>when</em> you use electricity. If your usage mirrors peak hours, your bill may rise. Use a bill comparison tool before switching.
          <h3>2. How do I know if my utility offers TOU?</h3>
          Check your utility’s website or call customer service. In many states (e.g., California, Texas, New York), utilities <em>must</em> offer TOU as an option. Look for plans named “TOU,” “Time of Use,” or “Smart Rate.”
          <h3>3. What happens if I don’t switch to TOU?</h3>
          You’ll stay on your current plan (usually a flat or tiered rate). In most cases, you can switch back and forth once per billing cycle — but policies vary. Some utilities charge a small fee for switching.
          <h3>4. Do TOU rates apply to solar customers?</h3>
          Yes — and they can be especially powerful. If you generate excess solar during the day, you sell it back at the peak or partial-peak rate (often higher than your TOU buyback rate). But you still pay peak rates for evening use. Net-metering rules affect this — check your state’s policy.
          <h3>5. Is there a risk of higher bills during summer?</h3>
          Yes. Summer peak hours often last longer (e.g., 1–8 pm vs. 2–7 pm in winter), and AC demand spikes. Many utilities apply a <strong>summer surcharge</strong> — so your peak rate in July may be 30% higher than in January. Plan ahead by shifting laundry and pool pumping to early morning.
          <h3>6. Can I get a smart meter to track TOU usage?</h3>
          Yes — utilities install them automatically when replacing old meters. You’ll get hourly usage data in your online account. Some even send alerts when you enter peak hours.
