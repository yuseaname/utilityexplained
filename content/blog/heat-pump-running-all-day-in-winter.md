<h2 id="problem">The problem: it seems like the heat pump never stops</h2>
<p class="lead">
              Your heat pump has been running for hours. The house is warm, but the
              system never seems to shut off. It feels like something is wrong.
            </p>
            <p>
              In winter, long runtimes can be normal because heat pumps move heat
              instead of making it. Still, there is a line between normal and
              inefficient. This guide helps you tell the difference.
            </p>

<h2 id="quick-answer">The short answer</h2>
<p>
              A heat pump can run for long stretches in winter because it is
              extracting heat from cold outdoor air. Longer runtime does not always
              mean a problem, especially during freezing weather.
            </p>
            <p>
              If the home never reaches setpoint or the air feels cold all day, it
              is time to dig deeper.
            </p>

<h2 id="why-long">Why heat pumps run longer in cold weather</h2>
<p>
              Heat pumps are most efficient in mild temperatures. When it is cold
              outside, they can still heat, but they need more runtime to deliver
              the same warmth.
            </p>
            <p>
              Think of it like carrying buckets in the snow. You can still move
              water, but it takes longer. That is why longer cycles can be normal.
            </p>
            <p>
              If you want the efficiency basics, see
              <a href="/blog/afue-vs-seer-vs-hspf-vs-cop"> HVAC efficiency ratings explained</a>.
            </p>

<h2 id="defrost">Defrost cycles and cool air</h2>
<p>
              In freezing weather, outdoor coils can ice up. The system enters a
              defrost cycle to clear that ice. During defrost, you might feel cooler
              air indoors for a few minutes.
            </p>
            <p>
              That brief cool air is normal. If it happens constantly, it may point
              to a different issue.
            </p>

<h2 id="aux-heat">Aux heat and emergency heat</h2>
<p>
              Many heat pumps have auxiliary heat that kicks in when outdoor
              temperatures drop too low or when the system needs help catching up.
            </p>
            <p>
              If you see "AUX" or "EMER" on your thermostat often, learn what those
              modes mean here:
              <a href="/blog/aux-heat-vs-emergency-heat-meaning"> aux heat vs emergency heat</a>.
            </p>

<h2 id="airflow">Airflow and filter checks</h2>
<p>
              Restricted airflow makes any heating system work harder. A clogged
              filter or blocked vent can force longer runtimes and weaker warmth.
            </p>
            <ul>
              <li>Replace filters on schedule.</li>
              <li>Keep return vents clear.</li>
              <li>Make sure supply vents are open.</li>
            </ul>

<h2 id="heat-loss">Heat loss and duct leaks</h2>
<p>
              If your home loses heat quickly, the heat pump will run more often to
              keep up. Air leaks, thin insulation, and leaky ducts are common causes.
            </p>
            <p>
              This guide helps you spot duct issues:
              <a href="/blog/duct-leaks-symptoms-hvac-running-all-day"> duct leaks symptoms</a>.
            </p>

<h2 id="signs">Signs it is not normal</h2>
<ul>
              <li>The home never reaches the thermostat setpoint.</li>
              <li>Indoor air feels cold for long stretches.</li>
              <li>Outdoor unit is iced over for hours.</li>
              <li>Energy use spikes far beyond typical winter patterns.</li>
            </ul>
            <p>
              If the air feels cold during heat mode, compare this guide:
              <a href="/blog/why-is-my-furnace-blowing-cold-air"> why a furnace blows cold air</a>.
            </p>

<h2 id="misconceptions">Common misconceptions</h2>
<ul>
              <li><strong>"Heat pumps should cycle like a furnace."</strong> They often run longer by design.</li>
              <li><strong>"Long runtime means it is failing."</strong> Not always. It can be normal in cold weather.</li>
              <li><strong>"Defrost means the system is broken."</strong> Defrost is a normal winter function.</li>
            </ul>

<h2 id="faqs">FAQs</h2>
<p>
              Quick answers to common heat pump runtime questions.
            </p>

{{< faq "Is it normal for a heat pump to run all day in winter?" >}}

It can be normal during very cold weather. Heat pumps often run longer to maintain the set temperature.

{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq "Why does my heat pump blow cool air sometimes?" >}}

During defrost cycles, the system can send cooler air indoors for a few minutes.

{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq "What does AUX heat mean?" >}}

AUX heat is backup heat that helps the heat pump when outdoor temperatures are low or the system needs to catch up.

{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq "When should I be concerned about nonstop runtime?" >}}

If the home never reaches setpoint, the air feels cold for long periods, or the outdoor unit stays iced over, it is worth checking.

{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq "Can duct leaks make a heat pump run longer?" >}}

Yes. Leaky ducts waste heated air and force the system to run longer to maintain comfort.

{{< /faq >}}
