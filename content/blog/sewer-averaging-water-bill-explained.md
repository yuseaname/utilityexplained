<h2 id="problem">The problem: your sewer charge stays high even when your summer use drops</h2>
<p class="lead">
              You cut back on outdoor watering, your water use goes down, but the sewer
              charge barely moves. That mismatch makes a lot of people think the bill
              is wrong.
            </p>
            <p>
              In most cities, the sewer line is not metered directly. Instead, the
              utility estimates your wastewater based on a winter average or a fixed
              seasonal formula. Once you understand that system, the charge makes more
              sense.
            </p>

              If you want the big picture first, start with
              <a href="/water-explained"> Water Service Explained</a>.
            </p>

<h2 id="quick-answer">The short answer</h2>
<p>
              <strong>Sewer averaging</strong> means your sewer charge is based on your
              <strong> winter water use</strong>, not your current month. The logic is
              simple: winter use mostly goes down the drain, while summer use often
              goes outdoors.
            </p>
            <p>
              That is why your sewer line can stay steady even when your summer water
              use drops.
            </p>

<h2 id="how-it-works">How sewer averaging works</h2>
<p>
              Many utilities set your sewer charge based on average water use during a
              winter period, often called the winter quarter. That winter average is
              then used for the rest of the year.
            </p>
            <h3>Why winter?</h3>
            <p>
              Winter water use is mostly indoor. Indoor use usually becomes wastewater,
              so it is a decent proxy for sewer flow.
            </p>
            <h3>Different approaches utilities use</h3>
            <ul>
              <li><strong>Winter average:</strong> averages a set of winter bills.</li>
              <li><strong>Annual average:</strong> averages the last 12 months.</li>
              <li><strong>Fixed allowance:</strong> a set base plus usage beyond it.</li>
            </ul>
            <p>
              If you want a deeper breakdown of the sewer line itself, see
              <a href="/blog/how-sewer-charges-work-on-your-water-bill"> how sewer charges work</a>.
            </p>

<h2 id="where-show">Where it appears on the bill</h2>
<p>
              Sewer lines are often labeled as "Sewer", "Wastewater", or "Sanitary".
              Some bills show a separate sewer usage quantity (CCF or gallons), even
              though that number is calculated, not directly metered.
            </p>
            <p>
              If the unit is unfamiliar, start with
              <a href="/blog/what-is-ccf-on-a-water-bill"> what CCF means on a water bill</a>.
            </p>
            <p>
              Your bill may also include a separate base fee. That is a different line
              than sewer averaging. See
              <a href="/blog/water-service-charge-explained"> water service charges explained</a>.
            </p>

<h2 id="what-raises">What can raise your average</h2>
<p>
              The winter average can creep up when something unusual happens during
              the averaging period.
            </p>
            <ul>
              <li><strong>Silent leaks:</strong> a running toilet can inflate the average.</li>
              <li><strong>Move-in spikes:</strong> new occupants often use more water at first.</li>
              <li><strong>Outdoor use in winter:</strong> rare, but it counts if it happens.</li>
              <li><strong>Estimated reads:</strong> a correction later can raise the average.</li>
            </ul>
            <p>
              If you suspect a leak, check
              <a href="/blog/water-meter-leak-indicator-explained"> the water meter leak indicator</a>
              or the quick test in
              <a href="/blog/is-your-toilet-running-leak-test"> is your toilet running</a>.
            </p>

<h2 id="estimate">How to estimate your sewer charge</h2>
<p>
              Find your winter usage average in CCF or gallons, then multiply it by
              your sewer rate per unit. If the bill shows a base fee, add it after
              the usage portion.
            </p>
            <p>
              This simple estimate helps you separate a sewer average issue from a
              sudden usage change. For a full troubleshooting flow, see
              <a href="/blog/why-did-my-water-bill-suddenly-increase"> why your water bill suddenly increased</a>.
            </p>

<h2 id="misconceptions">Common misconceptions</h2>
<ul>
              <li><strong>"Sewer should drop when watering stops."</strong> Not if the sewer charge is based on winter average.</li>
              <li><strong>"Sewer is measured directly."</strong> It is usually calculated from water use.</li>
              <li><strong>"My sewer line is a mistake."</strong> It is a normal part of most bills.</li>
            </ul>

<h2 id="faqs">FAQs</h2>
<p>
              Short answers to the most common sewer averaging questions.
            </p>

{{< faq "Why is my sewer charge the same every month?" >}}

Many utilities set sewer charges based on a winter average or a fixed allowance, so the sewer line stays steady even when monthly water use changes.

{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq "Does sewer averaging mean I am paying for summer watering?" >}}

Not directly. The idea is to exclude outdoor summer use by basing the sewer charge on winter indoor usage.

{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq "How do I find my winter average?" >}}

Look for a sewer usage line on your bill or check the winter months used for averaging. Some utilities list the averaged usage directly.

{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq "What if I had a leak during the averaging period?" >}}

A leak can raise the winter average. If you can document a repair, some utilities will review the average, but policies vary by provider.

{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq "Is sewer averaging the same everywhere?" >}}

No. Some utilities use winter averages, others use annual averages or fixed formulas. Your bill or local utility site usually explains the method.

{{< /faq >}}
