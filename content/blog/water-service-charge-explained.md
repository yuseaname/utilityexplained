<h2 id="problem">The problem: your bill has a charge even when you used almost no water</h2>
<p class="lead">

              bill looks tiny. And yet there it is: a water "service charge," "base

              That moment is frustrating because it feels like you are paying for
              nothing.
            </p>
            <p>

              you how to sanity-check what you are being charged.

<h2 id="quick-answer">The quick answer</h2>
<p>
              A <strong>water service charge</strong> is a <strong>fixed monthly fee</strong>
              that helps pay for keeping your account and connection "ready to serve"
              even if you use very little water.
            </p>
            <p>
              It is different from your <strong>usage charge</strong> (the part based on
              gallons, CCF, or kgal). It is also different from <strong>sewer</strong>,
              which is often calculated using your water usage as a proxy.
            </p>
            <p>
              If you are trying to lower your bill, the important takeaway is simple:
              <strong> you can usually lower usage, but the fixed fee stays the same</strong>
              unless your utility allows changes like a smaller meter size.
            </p>

<h2 id="what-it-is">What a water service charge is (and what it is not)</h2>
<p>
              The fixed fee goes by different names:
            </p>
            <ul>
              <li>Service charge</li>
              <li>Base charge</li>
              <li>Customer charge</li>
              <li>Meter charge</li>
              <li>Minimum charge (sometimes)</li>
            </ul>
            <p>
              The key idea is not the label. The key idea is what it does:
              <strong> it is not a measurement of water you used</strong>.
            </p>
            <p>
              Your usage is measured separately, usually in <strong>gallons</strong> or
              <strong> CCF</strong>. If you keep seeing "CCF" on your bill and want to
              translate it into something you can picture, see:
              <a href="/blog/what-is-ccf-on-a-water-bill"> what CCF means on a water bill</a>.
            </p>

<h2 id="what-it-covers">What the fixed fee usually covers</h2>
<p>
              Think of the fixed fee as the cost of having an active utility account
              and a working connection. Even if your usage is low, the system still
              exists for you.
            </p>
            <p>
              What it can include varies by city, but these are common:
            </p>
            <ul>
              <li>
                <strong>Meter costs</strong> (the meter itself, testing, replacement)
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>Billing and customer service</strong> (account setup, statements,
                payment processing)
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>Operations and maintenance</strong> (keeping pipes, valves, and
                pumps working)
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>Readiness costs</strong> (the system is sized and staffed so water
                is available when you need it)
              </li>
            </ul>
            <p>
              Some utilities also include separate fixed fees for things like "fire
              line," "hydrant," or "infrastructure." Those are still fixed-fee ideas,
              just broken into multiple lines.
            </p>

<h2 id="where-on-bill">Where to find the service charge on your bill</h2>
<p>
              The service charge usually shows up in the section that looks like a
              mini-itemized list. Look for a line that:
            </p>
            <ul>
              <li>Is the same amount each month, or</li>
              <li>Mentions "base," "service," "customer," or "meter," and</li>
              <li>Does not change when your gallons/CCF change</li>
            </ul>
            <p>
              If your bill includes sewer on the same statement, do not be surprised
              if it looks bigger than the water service charge. Sewer is often a
              major part of the total. This is the clearest explanation on the site:
              <a href="/blog/how-sewer-charges-work-on-your-water-bill"> how sewer charges work</a>.
            </p>

<h2 id="sanity-check">How to sanity-check the charge</h2>
<p>
              You do not need to be a rates expert to do a basic check. Use this
              three-step approach:
            </p>
            <h3>1) Confirm it is fixed (not usage-based)</h3>
            <p>
              Compare two bills: one with higher usage and one with lower usage. If
              the line item stays the same, it is a fixed fee.
            </p>
            <h3>2) Check whether the charge scales with meter size</h3>
            <p>
              Many utilities tie the service charge to meter size (for example, 5/8",
              3/4", 1"). A larger meter can mean a higher fixed charge.
            </p>
            <h3>3) Make sure you are not seeing "two fixed fees" that are actually different</h3>
            <p>
              It is common to have more than one fixed-fee-looking line item (water
              service + sewer base fee, or water base fee + infrastructure fee). If
              the labels are unclear, look for the section headings that separate
              "Water\" and \"Sewer."
            </p>
            <p>
              If your total jumped unexpectedly and you are trying to figure out
              whether it is usage, a rate change, or something else, this guide is a
              good next stop:
              <a href="/blog/why-did-my-water-bill-suddenly-increase"> why your water bill suddenly increased</a>.
            </p>

<h2 id="lower-total">What you can (and cannot) lower</h2>
<p>
              This is the part most people care about, so let's be direct.
            </p>
            <h3>You can usually lower</h3>
            <ul>
              <li>
                <strong>Usage charges</strong> by reducing gallons/CCF (fix leaks,
                adjust irrigation, shorten showers)
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>Summer spikes</strong> by focusing on outdoor use (see our
                summer guide below)
              </li>
            </ul>
            <h3>You usually cannot lower</h3>
            <ul>
              <li>
                <strong>The base/service charge</strong> without a specific program or
                account change
              </li>
            </ul>
            <p>
              Two practical next reads, depending on what you are seeing:
            </p>
            <ul>
              <li>
                If you suspect a hidden leak, use your meter as a truth source:
                <a href="/blog/water-meter-leak-indicator-explained"> water meter leak indicator explained</a>.
              </li>
              <li>
                If your bill climbs every summer, the pattern is usually outdoor use +

                <a href="/blog/why-is-my-water-bill-higher-in-summer"> why water bills are higher in summer</a>.
              </li>
            </ul>

<h2 id="misconceptions">Common misconceptions</h2>
<ul>
              <li>
                <strong>"The service charge means they estimated my usage."</strong>
                No. Estimated usage is about meter reads. The service charge is a
                fixed fee.
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>"If I used zero water, I should pay zero."</strong> It feels
                fair, but utilities are built around fixed system costs + variable
                usage costs.
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>"The fixed fee is the same everywhere."</strong> It is not.
                Utilities set rates locally, and the names and breakdowns differ.
              </li>
            </ul>

<h2 id="next-steps">Next steps if it still feels wrong</h2>
<p>
              If the charge is truly surprising, here are the calm, high-signal checks
              to make:
            </p>
            <ol>
              <li>
                <strong>Confirm the account status</strong> (active vs vacant vs seasonal).
                Some cities have special rates for vacant properties.
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>Confirm meter size</strong>. If the fixed fee is tied to meter size,
                make sure your record matches what is actually installed.
              </li>
              <li>
                <strong>Separate water vs sewer</strong>. Many "water bills" are really two
                services on one statement.
              </li>
            </ol>

{{< faq "What is a water service charge?" >}}

A water service charge is a fixed fee that helps cover the cost of maintaining the water system and keeping your service connection and account active, even if you use very little water.

{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq "Why do I pay a water base charge every month?" >}}

Most utilities bill a base/service charge to cover fixed costs like meters, billing, maintenance, and system readiness. Your usage charges are billed separately based on gallons/CCF.

{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq "Is the water service charge based on how much water I use?" >}}

Usually no. It is typically a fixed amount that does not change with your usage. Your usage charge changes based on the amount of water recorded by your meter.

{{< /faq >}}

{{< faq "Can I lower my water service charge?" >}}

In most areas the service charge is set by the utility and stays the same for a given account type or meter size. You can usually lower the usage portion of your bill by reducing consumption and fixing leaks.

{{< /faq >}}
