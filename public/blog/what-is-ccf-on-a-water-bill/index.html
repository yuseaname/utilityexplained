What Is CCF on a Water Bill? (Cubic Feet Explained in Plain English)

Utility Explained December 25, 2025 4 min read

CCF shows up on many water bills. Here's what it means, how to convert it to gallons, and how it turns into charges.

Start here: what CCF means on a water bill

If you opened your water bill and saw "CCF," you are not alone. It is one of those utility terms that feels obvious to the utility and totally unclear to everyone else.

Here is the simple definition: CCF means "hundred cubic feet." It is a unit of volume--how much water flowed through your meter during the billing period.

This article gives you a plain-English conversion you can trust, plus a quick way to connect the CCF number to what you actually pay.

If you want the big picture first, start with our Water Service Explained pillar.

CCF vs cubic feet vs gallons (and why bills use different units)

Utilities measure water in volume. The most common units you'll see are gallons, cubic feet (ft3), and CCF.

  • 1 cubic foot = 7.48 gallons
  • 1 CCF = 100 cubic feet = 748 gallons

So if your bill says you used 6 CCF, that is about 4,488 gallons ($6 x 748$).

Some cities use kgal (thousand gallons) instead. The important thing is that the unit is just a way to count volume-- it does not change the actual amount of water you used.

Quick conversions you can memorize

If you want a quick mental shortcut, these are the most useful:

The goal isn't perfect math. The goal is being able to look at your bill and think, "Is this number in the right ballpark?"

How CCF turns into charges

Once your utility has a usage number, it applies your local rates. That rate structure can be simple or complicated depending on where you live.

Flat-rate pricing

With a flat rate, each CCF costs the same. Your usage charge is basically: CCF x rate.

Tiered pricing

With tiers (block pricing), the first chunk of usage is cheaper and later chunks are more expensive. That can make it look like your "price per unit" changed month to month.

If your bill also includes sewer, your water usage may drive that charge too. See how sewer charges work for the plain-English model.

How to sanity-check your CCF usage

The fastest way to build confidence is to connect three things: (1) your meter, (2) your bill, and (3) what your household is doing.

1) Compare the meter to the bill

Your bill lists a previous and current meter reading. Subtract to get usage. If you want the step-by-step, see how to read a water meter.

2) Check whether the jump makes sense

If your usage suddenly spiked, your next step is usually to rule out leaks--especially toilets. Our troubleshooting guide is a simple toilet leak test (it takes minutes).

3) Use "normal" as context (not as a rule)

Households vary, but having a rough benchmark helps. See average water usage per person for a practical range.

Common misconceptions about CCF

  • "CCF is water pressure." No--pressure is psi. CCF is strictly volume.
  • "CCF is a fee." CCF is the measurement. The fee comes from the rate applied to that measurement.
  • "The utility is hiding gallons." Usually it's just a standard unit that's convenient for billing.

How many gallons are in 1 CCF?

1 CCF (hundred cubic feet) is 100 cubic feet, which is about 748 gallons.

What does CCF stand for on a water bill?

CCF stands for hundred cubic feet. It is a volume unit used to measure how much water you used during the billing period.

Is CCF the same as a cubic foot?

No. One CCF equals 100 cubic feet. A cubic foot is the smaller base unit; CCF is a larger bundled unit used for billing.

Why does my bill use CCF instead of gallons?

Some utilities use cubic feet/CCF because many water meters record in cubic feet. Billing in CCF is a convenient way to summarize volume.
